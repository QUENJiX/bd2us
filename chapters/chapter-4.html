Chapter 4: Application Platforms: Common App, Scoir, ApplyTexas & The Others

Gone are the days of mailing thick paper applications (thankfully!). Today, you'll wage your application war online through various platforms. Think of them as the different social media profiles for colleges – some are ubiquitous like Facebook (Common App), others are more niche like LinkedIn (Scoir), and some are region-specific like a local community group (ApplyTexas). Mastering these platforms is step one to actually getting your info seen.

1. The Common Application (Common App): The Undisputed King

What it is: The most widely used platform, accepted by over 1,000 colleges and universities in the U.S. and beyond, including most private institutions and many public ones. It's likely where you'll spend most of your time.

Key Features:

  • Main Profile: Fill out your demographics, family info, education history (Grades 9-12), standardized test scores (SAT/ACT/EPTs), honors, and the infamous Activities section once.
  • Activities Section: 10 slots max. Describe your ECAs concisely (150 characters for description!). Use action verbs, quantify impact. (More on this in Chapter 9).
  • Personal Essay: The main 650-word essay is submitted here, going to all colleges you apply to via Common App. (More in Chapter 11).
  • College-Specific Supplements: Each college adds its own questions ("Why Us?", short answers) and sometimes requires additional essays within the Common App portal.
  • Recommender Invites: You'll invite your teachers and counselor through the platform.

The Catch: You can only add a maximum of 20 colleges to your Common App dashboard at any one time. If you plan to apply to more than 20 schools total (which might be necessary for aid-seeking internationals casting a wide net), you'll need to either remove schools after submitting or use other platforms.

Pro Tip: Start filling out the main sections EARLY. It takes longer than you think. Double-check everything for typos and consistency before submitting to any school.

2. Scoir (Formerly known as the Coalition Application - though many still call it that): The Underdog with Potential

What it is: A newer, less widely used platform than Common App, but required or preferred by a significant number of colleges (often those focused on access and affordability, but also includes some major universities).

Key Features:

  • Different Interface: Feels a bit more modern, allows uploading media to a "Locker" (videos, projects - use judiciously!).
  • Similar Sections: Collects largely the same info as Common App (profile, essays, activities).
  • No College Limit: Unlike Common App's 20-school cap, Scoir doesn't limit the number of colleges you can apply to through their platform. This is its main advantage if you're applying widely.

The Catch: Fewer colleges use Scoir exclusively. You'll likely need both Common App and Scoir if your list includes schools from both camps. Check each college's preferred/required platform.

Pro Tip: Explore the "Locker" feature, but only upload supplementary materials if they genuinely add significant value and aren't easily described elsewhere. Don't just dump random stuff there.

3. ApplyTexas: The Lone Star State's Domain

What it is: The centralized application system for public universities and some private ones within the state of Texas. If you're applying to schools like UT Austin, Texas A&M, University of Houston, etc., you'll likely use this.

Key Features:

  • Texas-Centric: Designed specifically for Texas schools.
  • Different Essays: Has its own set of essay prompts, separate from Common App. Be prepared to write new essays tailored to Texas.
  • Focus on State Residency (Less Relevant to You): Much of its structure is geared towards Texas residents, but international students use it too.

The Catch: Only useful for Texas schools. Competition for international spots (and especially aid) at top Texas public schools is fierce due to policies favoring in-state students.

Pro Tip: Research Texas schools' international aid policies carefully before investing time in ApplyTexas. Highlight any connections or specific interests related to Texas in your essays.

4. University of California (UC) Application: California Dreaming (or Scheming)

What it is: A separate application system used exclusively by the nine undergraduate campuses of the University of California system (e.g., UCLA, UC Berkeley, UC San Diego).

Key Features:

  • Unique Essays (Personal Insight Questions - PIQs): Requires answering 4 out of 8 specific prompts (350 words each). These are different from Common App essays.
  • No LORs: The UC system traditionally does not accept Letters of Recommendation.
  • Test-Blind (Currently): The UCs are currently test-blind, meaning they will not consider SAT/ACT scores even if submitted. (Verify this policy annually as things can change).
  • Activities Description: Has its own way of listing and describing activities.

The Catch: Only for UC schools. Like Texas, UC schools offer very limited need-based financial aid to international students, though some merit scholarships exist. Primarily for students who can afford near full-price.

Pro Tip: Focus heavily on the PIQs and detailed activity descriptions, as these are the main ways they evaluate you beyond grades.

5. Direct University Applications: The VIP Clubs

What it is: Some colleges, often highly selective ones like MIT and Georgetown, require you to use their own unique application portal found directly on their website.

The Strategy: If these schools are on your list, you have no choice but to use their system. Be prepared for unique questions, essay prompts, and interfaces.

Pro Tip: Create accounts for these portals early and explore their specific requirements. Don't leave them until the last minute, assuming they're like Common App.

Key Strategy: Consistency and Organization are Your Lifelines

  • One Master Document: Keep a spreadsheet or document listing every college, its required platform(s), deadlines, essay prompts, and login details.
  • Consistent Information: Ensure the information you enter (grades, activity descriptions, etc.) is consistent across all platforms you use. Discrepancies look sloppy or dishonest.
  • Track Submission Status: Mark off when you submit each part of the application (main form, supplements, recommenders submit, scores sent, etc.).
  • Save Everything: Keep copies of your essays and responses for each platform.

Bottom Line: You'll likely juggle multiple platforms. Stay organized, read instructions carefully for each one, and tailor your content (especially supplemental essays) accordingly. Don't assume one size fits all. Get comfortable with these digital interfaces – they hold the keys to your application's submission.
