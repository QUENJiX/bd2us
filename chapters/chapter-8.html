Chapter 8: English Proficiency Tests (EPT): Proving You Can Order Coffee AND Understand Lectures

Right, you speak English. Maybe you went to an English Medium school, maybe you devour Hollywood movies without subtitles, maybe your internal monologue sounds suspiciously like Benedict Cumberbatch. Doesn't matter. For almost every international student applying to U.S. colleges, you need to prove your English proficiency with a standardized test.

Think of it as a bureaucratic passport stamp. The colleges need official confirmation that you can not only survive ordering a burger ("Ekta cheeseburger den, please" probably won't cut it) but also actively participate in fast-paced class discussions, understand complex academic texts, and write coherent essays without sounding like Google Translate had a meltdown. Even if your O-Level English grade was stellar, they usually still want an EPT score.

The Bottom Line Up Front: Don't overthink this too much, but don't dismiss it either. It's a necessary hoop to jump through. Your goal is to get the required score efficiently and move on to the parts of the application that actually showcase you.

Choosing Your Weapon: TOEFL vs. IELTS vs. Duolingo

You have three main contenders in the EPT arena. Most U.S. colleges accept all three, but ALWAYS check the specific requirements for EACH college on your list. Don't assume.

TOEFL iBT (Test of English as a Foreign Language): The Old Guard

  • Format: Primarily computer-based, taken at a test center (though a Home Edition exists). Four sections: Reading, Listening, Speaking (into a microphone), Writing. Focuses heavily on academic English. Recently shortened to under 2 hours.
  • Scoring: Out of 120 (30 points per section).
  • Target Score: Competitive schools usually want 100+. Top-tier schools might look for 105+ or even 110+. Anything below 90 can be problematic for selective colleges.
  • Cost: Roughly $190 - $200+ USD. Ouch.
  • Availability: Test centers available in major cities like Dhaka, Chattogram. Need to book in advance.
  • Best For: Students comfortable with purely academic contexts, standardized test formats, and speaking into a mic instead of to a person.

IELTS Academic (International English Language Testing System): The Conversational Cousin

  • Format: Available computer-based or paper-based at test centers. Four sections: Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. The Speaking test is a face-to-face interview with a human examiner (can be less awkward for some). Also leans academic.
  • Scoring: On a 9-band scale (0-9). Scores are often reported per section and overall.
  • Target Score: Competitive schools usually require an overall score of 7.0+ or 7.5+. Top schools might prefer 8.0+. Individual section scores might also have minimums (e.g., no section below 7.0).
  • Cost: Roughly $240 - $260+ USD. Even more ouch. (Often reflects local currency conversion rates set by British Council/IDP).
  • Availability: Widely available test centers. Also need to book ahead. Often the go-to if also considering UK/Australian universities.
  • Best For: Students who prefer a live speaking interview and perhaps the option of a paper-based test for other sections.

Duolingo English Test (DET): The Fast, Affordable Disruptor

  • Format: Taken online, at home, anytime (requires stable internet, webcam, mic, quiet room). Adaptive test â€“ difficulty adjusts based on your answers. Mixes question types testing reading, writing, listening, speaking. Includes a short, ungraded video interview and writing sample sent to colleges alongside the score.
  • Scoring: Out of 160, in 5-point increments. Also provides subscores.
  • Target Score: Acceptance is widespread now. Competitive schools often look for 120+ or 125+. Top-tier may want 130+ or even 135+.
  • Cost: $59 USD (as of late 2024 - check current price). HUGE advantage. You can also send scores to unlimited schools for free.
  • Availability & Speed: Take it whenever you're ready. Results arrive within 48 hours (usually faster). Unbelievably convenient.
  • Best For: Budget-conscious students, those needing scores quickly, students comfortable with online testing and adaptive formats, those applying only to the US (where its acceptance is now very high).
  • The Catch? The integrated, adaptive format can feel unusual at first. Requires strict adherence to testing rules at home to avoid invalidation.

Which Test? Decision Time:

  • Check College Requirements: Seriously, this is step one. Some might still prefer TOEFL/IELTS, though DET is nearly ubiquitous now.
  • Consider Cost: Duolingo is the clear winner here by a massive margin.
  • Consider Convenience: Duolingo wins again (at home, fast results).
  • Consider Test Style: Prefer academic focus & structure (TOEFL)? Live interview (IELTS)? Fast, adaptive, integrated (Duolingo)?
  • Practice Tests: Try free practice materials for each format to see what clicks.

Realistic Goals: Hitting the Benchmark

Aim comfortably above the minimum requirement listed by your target colleges. If they say "minimum 100 TOEFL," aiming for 105 gives you a buffer. For DET, if the minimum is 120, aim for 125 or 130.

EPT Prep Tips: Ace It Without Memorizing the Dictionary

  • Practice Regularly: Use official practice materials (TOEFL/IELTS websites, Duolingo's free practice test). Consistency beats cramming.
  • Focus on All Skills: Don't just drill reading. Practice speaking clearly and coherently (record yourself!), writing well-structured short essays, and understanding different accents in listening sections.
  • Clarity Over Complexity: Don't try to use unnecessarily fancy words. Clear, accurate, well-organized communication is key. Simple sentences are better than complex ones full of errors.
  • Time Management: Practice under timed conditions. These tests move fast.
  • Get Used to Accents: Listen to diverse English speakers (podcasts, news from BBC, CNN, NPR, Australian ABC).

Fee Waivers for EPTs: Mostly a Myth (Except Maybe Duolingo)

  • TOEFL/IELTS: Waivers are extremely rare for international students. Plan to pay.
  • Duolingo: Because the fee is already low, formal waivers are less common. However, as mentioned, some colleges do provide voucher codes to interested students demonstrating need, especially if you engage with them (virtual events, emails). It never hurts to ask politely.

Bottom Line: It's Just a Gatekeeper

Don't let the EPT dominate your application prep. It's a requirement to demonstrate baseline proficiency. Pick the test that works best for you (logistically and financially), prepare adequately to hit your target score range, submit the score, and focus your main energy on your grades, essays, ECAs, and financial aid applications â€“ the things that truly differentiate you. Clear this hurdle and move on.
